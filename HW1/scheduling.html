Why Scheduling Matters

The CPU can only execute one instruction stream per core at a time, but many processes may be ready to run. The scheduler chooses which process gets CPU time next. A good policy improves responsiveness (apps feel snappy), fairness (no starvation), and throughput (more work completed).

Common Scheduling Algorithms

  • FCFS (First-Come, First-Served): runs jobs in arrival order. Simple, but a long job can delay everyone behind it.
  • SJF (Shortest Job First): prefers the smallest CPU bursts, often lowering average waiting time, but it needs estimates and can starve long jobs.
  • Round Robin: each ready process gets a small “time slice” repeatedly; improves interactivity but adds context-switch overhead.

What the OS Measures

  • Waiting time: time spent ready but not running.
  • Turnaround time: total time from arrival to completion.
  • Response time: how quickly the process first gets CPU time (important for UI apps).

Extra Detail

Preemptive vs. non-preemptive

In non-preemptive scheduling, a running process keeps the CPU until it blocks or finishes. In preemptive scheduling, the OS can interrupt a running process (for example, when its time slice ends) to keep the system responsive and fair.
